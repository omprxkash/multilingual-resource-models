\begin{abstract}
Natural language processing for African languages faces three compounding
challenges: extreme data scarcity, high linguistic diversity, and the
computational cost of deploying large multilingual models in resource-constrained
environments. This paper presents a unified study across three complementary
research directions that together address this problem space.
First, we benchmark zero-shot cross-lingual transfer from Kinyarwanda to
Kirundi on a 14-class news classification task, evaluating six architectures
spanning multilingual transformers and traditional neural networks, and
introduce a catastrophic forgetting metric to quantify source-language
degradation after target fine-tuning.
Second, we propose a Language-Independent Data Augmentation pipeline (LiDA)
combined with autoencoder-based embedding refinement and a novel Multi-Head
Attention Guided Ensemble (MAGE) classifier for low-resource sentiment
analysis on AfriSenti, achieving a 3.64 percentage-point improvement in
weighted F1 over the baseline.
Third, we distil AfroXLMR-Large into a compact six-layer student model
(AfroXLMR-Comet) via a hybrid loss combining soft-label KL divergence and
attention-map MSE matching, reducing model size by 87.7\% and inference
latency by 20$\times$ while retaining 89\% of teacher F1 across five
African languages.
All code, models, and datasets are released publicly.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Africa is home to more than 2,000 living languages, yet fewer than twenty
appear in mainstream natural language processing (NLP) benchmarks
\cite{b_adelani_afrisenti}. The resulting digital language divide means
that populations communicating in Kinyarwanda, Kirundi, Swahili, Hausa,
Igbo, and Yoruba are effectively excluded from modern language technologies
such as search, translation, and sentiment-aware social listening.

Three structural barriers underlie this gap. The first is \emph{data
scarcity}: annotated corpora for Bantu languages are orders of magnitude
smaller than those available for English or Mandarin. The second is
\emph{linguistic diversity}: even closely related languages such as
Kinyarwanda and Kirundi share only partial vocabulary, making direct
resource reuse unreliable. The third is \emph{computational asymmetry}:
state-of-the-art multilingual models like XLM-R Large contain over 550
million parameters, making deployment on edge devices or low-bandwidth
infrastructure prohibitive.

This paper presents three interconnected contributions, each targeting one
of these barriers and evaluated on publicly available African-language
benchmarks.

\textbf{Contribution 1 (Cross-Lingual Transfer)}: We systematically
benchmark zero-shot cross-lingual transfer from Kinyarwanda to the closely
related Kirundi language. We compare three multilingual transformer
variants—mBERT \cite{b_devlin}, AfriBERTa \cite{b_ogueji}, and
BantuBERTa \cite{b_dossou}—against three traditional architectures:
bidirectional GRU with Word2Vec embeddings, TextCNN \cite{b_kim},
and character-level CNN \cite{b_zhang}. We introduce a
\emph{catastrophic forgetting percentage} (CF\%) to quantify how much
a model degrades on the source language after being fine-tuned on the
target language.

\textbf{Contribution 2 (Data Augmentation)}: We propose MAGE
(Multi-Head Attention Guided Embeddings), a language-independent
augmentation and classification framework for low-resource African
sentiment analysis. MAGE applies LiDA (Language-Independent Data
Augmentation) in the embedding space, refines augmented embeddings
through denoising autoencoders (DAE) or variational autoencoders (VAE),
and classifies with a novel multi-head attention ensemble head.

\textbf{Contribution 3 (Knowledge Distillation)}: We compress
AfroXLMR-Large into AfroXLMR-Comet, a six-layer student model trained
with a hybrid distillation loss that simultaneously matches soft output
distributions (KL divergence, temperature $T{=}2$) and internal attention
maps (mean-pooled MSE). AfroXLMR-Comet achieves 87.7\% parameter
reduction and 20$\times$ faster inference on five African languages.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section~\ref{sec:related}
reviews related work; Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the datasets;
Section~\ref{sec:method} details the three methodologies;
Section~\ref{sec:results} reports experimental results;
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses findings; and
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Multilingual Pretrained Language Models}
mBERT \cite{b_devlin} and XLM-R \cite{b_conneau} demonstrated that a
single model trained on 100+ languages can transfer zero-shot across
languages sharing sub-word vocabulary. AfroXLMR \cite{b_alabi} further
adapted XLM-R to 17 African languages through continued multilingual
pre-training, while AfriBERTa \cite{b_ogueji} and BantuBERTa
\cite{b_dossou} are domain-specific models trained exclusively on African
corpora, enabling stronger intra-family transfer for Bantu languages.

\subsection{Cross-Lingual Transfer and Catastrophic Forgetting}
Zero-shot cross-lingual transfer leverages shared sub-word vocabularies
across typologically related languages \cite{b_conneau}. A persistent
challenge is catastrophic forgetting \cite{b_mccloskey,b_kirkpatrick}: when
a model is fine-tuned on a target language, it degrades on the source.
Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) \cite{b_kirkpatrick} penalises changes
to important parameters, while continual learning approaches \cite{b_devlin}
revisit the source corpus during target training. Our CF\% metric provides
a simple, interpretable quantification of this trade-off.

\subsection{Data Augmentation for Low-Resource NLP}
Easy Data Augmentation (EDA) \cite{b_wei} applies synonym replacement and
random insertion/deletion at the word level but requires language resources.
Back-translation \cite{b_sennrich} augments training data via machine
translation but is impractical when translation systems for the target
language do not exist. LiDA \cite{b_mage} operates entirely in the
embedding space, adding uniform random noise to sentence representations,
making it language-agnostic by design. Variational autoencoders
\cite{b_kingma} and denoising autoencoders \cite{b_vincent} have been
applied to embed-space augmentation to produce semantically coherent
synthetic examples.

\subsection{Knowledge Distillation}
Hinton et al.\ \cite{b_hinton} introduced knowledge distillation via soft
probability targets at temperature $T$. DistilBERT \cite{b_sanh} showed
that a six-layer BERT student retains 97\% of teacher performance at 40\%
fewer parameters. TinyBERT \cite{b_jiao} added transformer-layer and
attention-map matching, reducing parameters by 7.5$\times$. Our approach
follows the attention transfer idea of Zagoruyko and
Komodakis \cite{b_zagoruyko} but uses mean-pooled, flattened attention maps
with a learnable projection to bridge the teacher–student dimensional gap.

\section{Datasets}\label{sec:data}

\subsection{KINNEWS and KIRNEWS}
The KINNEWS and KIRNEWS corpora \cite{b_kinnews} consist of online news
articles in Kinyarwanda and Kirundi respectively, labelled across 14
categories (Politics, Sport, Economy, Health, Entertainment, History,
Technology, Tourism, Culture, Fashion, Religion, Environment, Education,
Relationship). Following \cite{b_thangaraj}, we drop the two smallest
classes (Fashion, Tourism) and remap remaining labels to a contiguous
0–11 range. Title and body are concatenated with a \texttt{[SEP]} token.
Statistics are shown in Table~\ref{tab:data}.

\subsection{AfriSenti-SemEval}
The AfriSenti dataset \cite{b_adelani_afrisenti} provides tweet-level
sentiment annotations (positive, negative, neutral) for eleven African
languages from the SemEval-2023 Shared Task 12. We use the Kinyarwanda,
Swahili, Xitsonga, Hausa, Igbo, and Yoruba subsets. Class distributions
are moderately imbalanced, with neutral tweets underrepresented in
smaller language subsets.

\section{Methodology}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Cross-Lingual Transfer Benchmark}\label{sec:xlt}

\subsubsection{Transformer Models}
We fine-tune three pretrained models on the Kinyarwanda training set and
evaluate on Kirundi without further adaptation (zero-shot) and after
fine-tuning on the Kirundi training set. All transformer experiments use
the HuggingFace \texttt{Trainer} \cite{b_wolf} with:
batch size 32, learning rate $5\times10^{-5}$, 8 epochs,
500 warm-up steps, weight decay 0.01, max sequence length 128.

\subsubsection{Traditional Neural Models}
\textbf{BiGRU}: A two-layer bidirectional GRU
\cite{b_cho} with 50-dimensional skip-gram Word2Vec
embeddings \cite{b_mikolov} trained on the combined Kinyarwanda and
Kirundi corpora ($\text{sg}=1$, $\text{hs}=1$, window 5).
Hidden dimension 256; dropout 0.5; trained for 10 epochs with Adam.

\textbf{TextCNN}: Kim (2014) architecture \cite{b_kim} with parallel
filters of sizes $\{3,4,5\}$ (150 filters each), max pooling, and the same
Word2Vec embeddings. Dropout 0.5; 8 epochs.

\textbf{CharCNN}: Zhang et al.\ (2015) architecture \cite{b_zhang} with a
68-character vocabulary, 1,500-character input length, six convolutional
layers (256 filters, kernel 7), two fully connected layers (1,024 units),
SGD optimiser, learning rate 0.01, 30 epochs.

\subsubsection{Catastrophic Forgetting Metric}
We define CF\% as:
\begin{equation}
    \text{CF\%} = \frac{\text{Acc}_{\text{src}}^{\text{before}} - \text{Acc}_{\text{src}}^{\text{after}}}
                       {\text{Acc}_{\text{src}}^{\text{before}}} \times 100
    \label{eq:cf}
\end{equation}
where $\text{Acc}_{\text{src}}$ is accuracy on the Kinyarwanda test set
measured before and after fine-tuning on Kirundi. Lower CF\% indicates
better knowledge retention.

\subsection{MAGE: Augmentation and Ensemble Classification}\label{sec:mage}

\subsubsection{Language-Independent Data Augmentation (LiDA)}
Given a sentence embedding $\mathbf{e} \in \mathbb{R}^{768}$ extracted
by AfriBERTa, LiDA generates an augmented copy by adding uniform noise:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\mathbf{e}} = \mathbf{e} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad
    \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{U}(r_{\min}, r_{\max})^{768}
    \label{eq:lida}
\end{equation}
We use $r_{\min}=0, r_{\max}=0.1$ and generate two augmented copies per
original, tripling the effective training set.

\subsubsection{Autoencoder Refinement}
Two variants refine the augmented embeddings before classification.

\textbf{DAE}: A denoising autoencoder ($768\to32\to768$) with LeakyReLU
activations, BatchNorm, and dropout 0.2 learns to reconstruct clean
embeddings from the LiDA-perturbed inputs.

\textbf{VAE}: A variational autoencoder ($768\to512\to256\to512\to768$)
with reparameterisation trick; the ELBO loss is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{VAE}} = \mathbb{E}[\|\hat{\mathbf{e}} - \mathbf{e}\|_2^2]
    + \beta \, D_{\text{KL}}(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2) \,\|\, \mathcal{N}(0,I))
    \label{eq:vae}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Multi-Head Attention Guided Ensemble (MAGE)}
The MAGE classifier takes a pooled embedding $\mathbf{e}$ and applies
$H=4$ independent attention heads, each with a learnable context vector
$\mathbf{c}_h \in \mathbb{R}^{768}$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{o}_h = \sigma(\mathbf{e}^\top \mathbf{c}_h) \cdot \mathbf{e},
    \quad
    \mathbf{z} = \sum_{h=1}^{H} \mathbf{o}_h
    \label{eq:mage}
\end{equation}
The aggregated representation $\mathbf{z}$ is passed through a two-layer
MLP classifier. Combining LiDA augmentation, DAE/VAE refinement, and MAGE
forms the full pipeline evaluated in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\subsection{AfroXLMR-Comet: Knowledge Distillation}\label{sec:distill}

\subsubsection{Teacher and Student Architecture}
The teacher (AfroXLMR-Large) and student (AfroXLMR-Comet) differ in every
dimension as summarised in Table~\ref{tab:arch}.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Property} & \textbf{Teacher} & \textbf{Student} \\
\midrule
Layers            & 24     & 6      \\
Hidden size       & 1,024  & 256    \\
Attention heads   & 16     & 8      \\
Intermediate size & 4,096  & 1,024  \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsubsection{Hybrid Distillation Loss}
The student is trained with a two-component loss. The \emph{response-based}
component uses KL divergence between softened output distributions:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{distill}} = T^2 \cdot D_{\text{KL}}
    \!\left( \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{z}_s}{T}\right) \,\Big\|\,
             \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{z}_t}{T}\right) \right)
    \label{eq:kl}
\end{equation}
The \emph{feature-based} component matches last-layer attention maps via
MSE after mean-pooling across heads and projecting student attention
to teacher dimensions:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{attn}} = \left\|\mathbf{W}\,
    \overline{\mathbf{A}}_s - \overline{\mathbf{A}}_t \right\|_2^2
    \label{eq:attn}
\end{equation}
where $\overline{\mathbf{A}} \in \mathbb{R}^{L\times L}$ is the mean-head
attention matrix and $\mathbf{W}$ is a learnable projection.
The combined loss balances both:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \alpha\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{distill}}
    + (1-\alpha)\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{attn}},\quad \alpha = 0.5
    \label{eq:total}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Training Protocol}
\textbf{Stage 1 (Distillation)}: AdamW, $\text{lr}=5\times10^{-5}$,
15 epochs, batch size 8, gradient accumulation 2 steps, FP16, early
stopping patience 3, temperature $T=2$.
\textbf{Stage 2 (Fine-tuning)}: Each language dataset is fine-tuned
independently: $\text{lr}=2\times10^{-5}$, 5 epochs.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Why does AfriBERTa outperform mBERT?}
AfriBERTa was pretrained exclusively on African corpora covering 11
languages, including several Bantu languages. Its sub-word vocabulary
consequently overlaps more with Kinyarwanda and Kirundi than mBERT's
vocabulary, which was built to cover 104 languages with the same 110k token
budget. This higher vocabulary alignment directly improves zero-shot
transfer accuracy (74.2\% vs.\ 58.7\%) and reduces catastrophic forgetting.

\textbf{Why do transformers resist catastrophic forgetting?}
Pre-trained representations distribute linguistic knowledge across all
parameters, making any single task fine-tune a small perturbation.
Traditional models store all task knowledge in the same embedding and
classifier weights, so target fine-tuning fully overwrites the source
signal. Our CF\% results (3–5\% for transformers vs.\ 73–75\% for
traditional models) quantify this difference precisely.

\textbf{Why does LiDA benefit low-resource languages?}
Unlike word-level augmentation methods, LiDA requires no dictionary, WordNet,
or translation system—resources unavailable for most Bantu languages. By
operating entirely in embedding space it is language-agnostic, and the
combination with autoencoder refinement removes noise introduced by the
perturbation while preserving semantic structure.

\textbf{Distillation limitations.}
Swahili shows the largest F1 gap between teacher and student (8.8 points).
This correlates with a smaller training set (3,009 sentences) that is
insufficient for the student to learn robust representations from the
teacher's soft targets alone. Future work could combine distillation with
LiDA augmentation for such low-data scenarios.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented a unified study of three complementary approaches to
low-resource African NLP. Across benchmarks spanning news classification
and tweet sentiment analysis in six African languages, our findings are:
(1)~AfriBERTa achieves the best cross-lingual transfer accuracy at 88.3\%
with only 5.14\% catastrophic forgetting;
(2)~MAGE with DAE augmentation improves sentiment F1 by 3.64 points over
the logistic regression baseline on AfriSenti;
(3)~AfroXLMR-Comet reduces model size by 87.7\% and inference time by 20$\times$
while retaining 89.2\% of teacher F1 across five languages.

Together, these results demonstrate that meaningful African-language NLP is
achievable without large compute budgets through targeted model selection,
language-independent augmentation, and efficient compression. Future work
will extend the framework to named entity recognition using MasakhaNER,
explore continual learning to eliminate catastrophic forgetting, and
investigate deploying AfroXLMR-Comet on edge devices for offline
communities.
